# Expert Rater Qualifications

**Version:** 1.0
**Date:** 2026-04-10
**Study:** Prompt Compression x Clinical Fidelity (Phase 0 -> Phase 1)
**Purpose:** Define minimum qualifications, target N, compensation, recruitment channels, and ideal profile for the expert raters who will blind-evaluate simulated therapeutic conversations in Hebrew.

---

## 1. Background and the question this document answers

Phase 0 of the Prompt Compression study asks expert clinicians to blind-rate 20 simulated client conversations (10 under a 33K prompt, 10 under a 3K prompt) on clinical fidelity, realism, and therapeutic usefulness. The simulations are in Hebrew, the client persona is depressed (unipolar, moderate severity), and the therapist inputs span four skill tiers (novice, intermediate, skilled, harmful). Raters need enough clinical expertise to recognize both (a) whether Sara behaves like a real depressed Israeli client and (b) whether her response pattern is clinically appropriate to the therapist's skill level.

This document answers eight methodological questions and then distills them into concrete recommendations for recruitment, compensation, and ethics.

---

## 2. Findings from the methodological literature

### Q1. What are the minimum qualifications typically required for raters in clinical psychology research using expert evaluation?

Consensus recommendations on rater training and certification for clinical trials (Kobak et al., 2005; Demitrack et al., 2013) emphasize two things: **(a) demonstrated competence with the specific rating scale** (via calibration exercises against expert-scored benchmarks) and **(b) a baseline of clinical knowledge of the disorder being assessed.** Formal licensure is not always required, but a background in mental health is the norm in published competence-rating studies. Non-clinician raters are acceptable when they receive structured training and achieve reliability against expert benchmarks, but that is a compromise option, not the default.

For psychotherapy competence ratings specifically (CTRS, CTS-R, ENACT, QACP), the standard design uses **licensed clinicians or advanced trainees supervised by a licensed clinician**. Muse & McManus (2013) and the Introducing the QACP paper (Muse et al., 2022) both use raters who are qualified CBT therapists or doctoral-level trainees.

### Q2. How many years of experience is typical?

The most commonly cited range in published CBT competence-rating studies is **3 to 10 years of post-qualification clinical experience.** The Cognitive Therapy Rating Scale (CTRS) reliability literature frequently reports raters with ~5 years of CBT experience. The Competence Ratings in Psychotherapy Training review (Muse & McManus / Springer 2023) describes cohorts of licensed clinical psychologists with 3-9 years of experience trained for roughly 12 hours before rating. Shorter experience can work if training is longer and benchmarks are tighter; longer experience allows lighter training.

### Q3. What licensing/credentials matter? (licensed clinical psychologist vs. psychiatrist vs. therapist vs. student)

Ranked in descending order of strength for **this** study (depression, CBT context, Hebrew):

1. **Licensed clinical psychologist (Israeli Ministry of Health, "Pinkas Hapsychologim" + clinical specialization)** -- the gold standard. In Israel this requires a Master's or higher in psychology, registration in the Pinkas Hapsychologim, and recognition as a specialist by the Moetset Hapsychologim's clinical subcommittee. Minimum training time from BA to full clinical specialization is ~11 years.
2. **Licensed psychiatrist with psychotherapy training.** Strong for depression severity judgments but weaker for therapeutic process (unless they have explicit psychotherapy training, which is not universal in Israeli psychiatry).
3. **Advanced clinical psychology trainee ("mitmachim klini'im")** in their final year of clinical specialization, supervised by a licensed clinical psychologist. Acceptable as a secondary tier if paired with expert benchmarks.
4. **Licensed social worker (MSW) with a psychotherapy specialization** ("oved sotziali kliini"). Acceptable in Israel and commonly used in mental health services; appropriate if they have depression and CBT experience.
5. **Clinical psychology doctoral students or early MA trainees.** Only as a fallback tier, and only with explicit training + calibration exercises against an expert benchmark.

For the **CBT/depression** emphasis, what matters more than licensing category is (a) experience treating unipolar depression and (b) familiarity with a structured CBT framework. A licensed psychodynamic therapist with no CBT exposure would not be an ideal rater even though they are technically more senior than a CBT-trained trainee.

### Q4. What knowledge of the specific modality matters (CBT for depression, therapeutic simulation training, Hebrew clinical language)?

Three overlapping knowledge domains are needed:

- **CBT for depression:** raters should recognize Beckian cognitive distortions, behavioral activation rationale, and the rhythm of structured CBT sessions. Familiarity with the CTRS-R construct space is a plus even if the scale itself is not used. The Beck Institute's CTRS-R is the field standard.
- **Therapeutic simulation / standardized patient methodology:** helpful but not required. Most clinicians are not trained in SP evaluation, and this is itself a reason to include calibration exercises. Raters who have trained residents or supervised role-plays will adapt fastest.
- **Hebrew clinical language:** **required, non-negotiable for this study.** The stimuli are in Hebrew and rely on nuanced body-language descriptions ("מבט מזוגג", "קול שטוח", "עוטפת את עצמה", etc.). A rater who needs a translation loses the body-language signal entirely. Raters must be first-language-proficient Hebrew clinicians who practice in Israel.

### Q5. What is the typical N for expert-rater studies like this one?

For **calibration / reliability phases** of psychotherapy competence scales, published studies commonly use **3-6 raters** (e.g., CTS-R and ENACT training studies). The present plan of **3-5 raters** is within the normal range and is defensible. For a Phase 0 pilot whose primary aim is effect-size estimation (not a definitive reliability study), **3 raters is the minimum; 4-5 is safer.** The Wasserman "Effective number of subjects and number of raters" literature argues that once you have a reasonable rater pool, additional subjects (conversations) give you more ICC precision than additional raters do -- so the 20-conversation x 3-5 rater design is well-balanced.

### Q6. What inter-rater reliability targets are typical? (ICC > 0.6? 0.7?)

Two dominant interpretive frameworks:

- **Cicchetti (1994):** <.40 poor, .40-.59 fair, .60-.74 good, .75-1.00 excellent.
- **Koo & Li (2016):** <.50 poor, .50-.75 moderate, .75-.90 good, >.90 excellent.

For **published** psychotherapy competence studies, the CTRS-R typically achieves **ICC total-score ~.75-.90** after training, with individual items .60-.85. For a Phase 0 pilot the realistic target is:

- **Primary global quality ratings: ICC(2,k) >= 0.70** (good-to-moderate, acceptable for research).
- **Individual dimension ratings (e.g., realism, body-language, therapist-responsiveness): ICC(2,k) >= 0.60** acceptable, **>= 0.70** preferred.
- **Forced-choice comparisons (33K vs 3K pair):** kappa >= 0.60 (substantial agreement).

If reliability falls below .60 on any dimension, the plan is to hold a calibration meeting, discuss discrepancies, and re-rate a subset. If it fails again, the dimension is treated as unreliable and excluded from primary analysis.

### Q7. How should raters be compensated? (honorarium, acknowledgment, coauthorship)

Three ethically defensible models; pick one per rater:

1. **Honorarium** -- flat payment for completing the rating protocol. Based on Israeli norms for clinician consulting time and on the ~3-4 hour rating load per rater, a defensible rate is **~200-300 ILS per hour or a flat 800-1,200 ILS honorarium per rater.** Honoraria are classed as "compensation for time and effort" and raise the fewest ethics concerns when kept modest (so as not to constitute undue inducement).
2. **Acknowledgment** -- named thanks in the manuscript's acknowledgments section, no payment. Appropriate for senior colleagues who decline payment and for raters contributing a small amount of time (<2 hours).
3. **Co-authorship** -- reserved for raters who contribute **substantial intellectual input beyond rating**: scale development, interpretation of discrepancies, writing sections of the manuscript. Per ICMJE authorship criteria, rating alone does NOT qualify for authorship. If a rater contributes to methodology design, inter-rater calibration discussion, and interpretation of findings, then co-authorship is appropriate.

The recommendation for this study is a **hybrid: honorarium for all raters + acknowledgment for all raters + co-authorship only for any rater who crosses the ICMJE threshold** (which should be stated explicitly in the recruitment email so expectations are clear from the start).

### Q8. IRB/ethics considerations for recruiting clinicians as raters.

Key considerations from the IRB literature and Israeli ethics norms:

- **Raters are research participants too.** Even though they are contributing expertise, they are providing data (their ratings) and must give informed consent. An IRB protocol should cover them as a participant class.
- **No clinical vulnerability / no coercion.** Recruiting clinicians is low-risk because they are not patients, but the researcher must avoid recruiting clinicians over whom they have supervisory authority (e.g., PhD students of the PI), or if they do, must disclose and document that the rater's decision to participate has no bearing on their training or employment. In Israel, recruiting from a single institution where the PI has influence should be flagged in the IRB application.
- **Confidentiality of rater identity.** Raters should be told whether their ratings are anonymous to each other, whether individual ratings will be reported, and whether they will see others' ratings during calibration.
- **Data sensitivity.** The conversations themselves are synthetic (no real patient data), which sharply lowers the ethics burden. This should be stated explicitly in the rater consent form: *"You will rate entirely synthetic simulated conversations. No real patient data is used at any point."* This is the single most important sentence for getting IRB approval quickly.
- **Honorarium disclosure.** The amount must be stated in the recruitment email and the consent form, framed as "compensation for your time" rather than "payment for services" to stay within the honorarium frame.
- **Right to withdraw.** Raters must be told they can withdraw at any time without penalty and that partial ratings will not be used without their permission.

The combination of (synthetic data + licensed-professional participants + modest honorarium + clear consent) should qualify for **expedited IRB review** at most Israeli institutions (BIU, HUJI, TAU, BGU ethics committees).

## 3. Recommendations for THIS study

### Minimum qualifications (non-negotiable)

1. **Active Israeli clinical license** -- licensed clinical psychologist (preferred), licensed clinical social worker with psychotherapy specialization, or licensed psychiatrist with psychotherapy training.
2. **>= 3 years of post-license clinical practice** treating adult depression.
3. **First-language proficiency in Hebrew** and current clinical practice in Hebrew.
4. **Familiarity with CBT**, even if it is not the rater's primary modality. A rater who has never heard of behavioral activation is not eligible.
5. **No authorship or supervisory conflict** with the PI (Elad Refoua) on the current study.

### Preferred qualifications (bonus)

- 5+ years of practice.
- Experience supervising trainees or evaluating role-plays.
- Familiarity with the CTRS-R, CTS-R, or other psychotherapy competence scales.
- Prior participation in psychotherapy process research.
- Experience with simulation or standardized-patient methodology.

### Target N

- **Primary target: 5 raters.** This gives headroom for one dropout and keeps ICC(2,k) estimates stable.
- **Minimum acceptable: 3 raters.**
- **Do not exceed 7** for Phase 0 -- the marginal reliability gain beyond 5 raters does not justify the scheduling and calibration overhead.

### Recruitment channels

1. **Personal and lab-adjacent clinicians** who are NOT in a supervisory relationship with the PI. Specifically: clinicians who have collaborated on MENTI or mentiverse work in a consultation-only capacity.
2. **The Israeli CBT association (Itamar, the Israeli Society for Cognitive and Behavior Therapies).** A short call for raters via the association's mailing list typically yields several responses from mid-career clinicians.
3. **Clinical psychology departments at BIU, HUJI, TAU, BGU** via direct colleague outreach (not mass email to students).
4. **Clinicians from the Rafaeli lab's professional network** who are not current collaborators on the paper.

Avoid: (a) mass recruitment via Facebook groups (self-selection bias), (b) paying a recruitment service, (c) any rater who has previously seen the Sara persona.

### Compensation package (recommended)

- **Honorarium: 1,000 ILS per rater** for the full protocol (estimated 3-4 hours of work including calibration meeting).
- **Acknowledgment:** every rater named in the manuscript's acknowledgments.
- **Co-authorship:** offered only if the rater contributes beyond rating (methodology discussion, manuscript input).
- **Access:** raters get a copy of the preprint and results summary as soon as they are available.

### IRB submission checklist (for the project's ethics application)

- [ ] Rater class included as "expert raters" participant group.
- [ ] Explicit statement that all rated conversations are synthetic.
- [ ] Rater consent form (separate from client/patient consent since there are no patients).
- [ ] Honorarium amount and framing disclosed.
- [ ] Conflict-of-interest statement: no rater supervises or is supervised by the PI.
- [ ] Data handling: rater identity kept separate from ratings in analysis files; rater names published only in acknowledgments with their permission.
- [ ] Right to withdraw clause.
- [ ] Expedited review requested.

## 4. Profile of the ideal rater

The ideal rater is:

- A **licensed clinical psychologist in Israel** with **5-10 years of post-license practice**, who treats adult depression as a regular part of their caseload.
- Practices in **Hebrew** and is first-language-proficient.
- Has **CBT training** (even if not their exclusive modality) and understands behavioral activation, cognitive restructuring, and Beckian formulation.
- Has **experience supervising trainees** or has participated in role-play based training, so they have calibrated intuitions about what realistic vs. caricatured client behavior looks like.
- Is **not a current collaborator** on this paper and has **no supervisory relationship** with the PI.
- Can commit to **3-4 hours of work** over a 2-3 week window: ~30 min onboarding + calibration, ~2-2.5 hours of rating the 20 stimuli, ~30-45 min post-rating discussion.
- Has **no prior exposure to the Sara persona** or to the MENTI simulation platform as a user.

One rater on the panel should additionally have **AI/LLM exposure** (has used ChatGPT or a similar tool and understands what "prompted simulation" means) so they can sanity-check the framing of the study. This is a nice-to-have, not a requirement.

## 5. Alternative tiers if ideal raters are unavailable

### Tier A -- ideal (target)
5 licensed clinical psychologists, 5+ years of practice, CBT-familiar, Hebrew-first.

### Tier B -- acceptable with calibration
Mix of 2-3 licensed clinical psychologists (3+ years) + 1-2 licensed clinical social workers or licensed psychiatrists with psychotherapy training. All Hebrew-first. Add 1-hour pre-rating calibration session using 2 practice conversations scored by the PI + a senior clinical collaborator.

### Tier C -- fallback, minimum viable
3 raters: at least 2 licensed clinicians (any mental health license) + 1 advanced clinical psychology trainee (final year of "hitmachut klini"). All Hebrew-first. Requires 2-hour pre-rating calibration and a mid-protocol reliability check; any dimension with ICC < .50 is dropped before final analysis.

### Tier D -- NOT acceptable (documented so no one drifts into this by accident)
- Undergraduate psychology students.
- Clinicians who do not practice in Hebrew.
- Any rater supervised by or supervising the PI.
- AI/LLM evaluators substituted for clinicians (these are part of the secondary judge panel, not a replacement for expert raters).
- Raters who have seen the Sara persona or MENTI platform in a user capacity.

If recruitment cannot reach Tier C, the **study is delayed**, not run with weaker raters. Fabricating qualifications is not an option; the alternative to qualified raters is waiting longer, not publishing weaker data.

---

## 6. Operational notes for recruitment

- Target recruitment window: ~3 weeks from first contact to last rater confirmed.
- Expect ~30-50% response rate to initial outreach; plan to contact 10-15 candidates to land 5 confirmed raters.
- Use a brief (1-page) recruitment email that states: purpose, time commitment, honorarium, synthetic-data disclaimer, how to decline.
- Send a follow-up email 4-5 days after initial contact; do not pressure.
- Once confirmed, send the consent form, the rating instructions, and the 20 stimuli as a single bundle. Do not drip-feed the stimuli (it inflates effort for no benefit).
- Schedule one **optional** 60-minute calibration Zoom meeting after the first 4 conversations have been rated. Attendance is encouraged but not required; raters who cannot attend get a written summary.
- Collect ratings via the evaluation platform in `platform/` (see `research/prompt-compression-study/platform/`); do not email ratings back and forth.
