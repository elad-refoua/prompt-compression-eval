## 1. Background and Rationale

Current best practice for AI-based therapeutic simulations used in clinical training relies on long, structured prompts -- typically 25,000 to 35,000 characters -- that encode a clinical persona, defense mechanisms, trigger-response contingencies, non-linear emotional trajectories, body-language banks, and phase-gated disclosure rules. Authoring such a prompt requires days of expert clinical work per persona. This high cost places a hard ceiling on how many distinct simulations a training program can offer, limits the diversity of presentations, and effectively locks simulation-based training out of training programs without dedicated clinical prompt-engineering expertise.

This study tests whether a compact 3,000-character "persona card" can produce simulations of clinically equivalent quality to those produced by the full-length prompt. The persona card retains the identity, core beliefs, defense mechanism names, trigger map, key thought chains, voice markers, threshold turns, and one explicit meta-rule about non-linearity. The universal simulation architecture (how defense mechanisms behave, how non-linearity works, what phase gates mean) is assumed to be implicitly available in the base model's prior knowledge rather than spelled out in each prompt.

If this compression works, persona creation drops from days of expert clinical work to roughly one hour of clinical formulation, democratizing access to high-quality clinical simulations. If it does not work, the study identifies precisely which architectural features are critical and which are expendable -- equally valuable information for refining clinical authoring effort.

## 2. Research Questions and Hypotheses

**Primary research question (RQ1):** Do expert clinical raters judge AI-generated therapeutic simulations produced from a 3K persona card as clinically equivalent to those produced from the full 33K prompt, across multiple dimensions of clinical fidelity?

**Secondary research question (RQ2):** Can expert clinical raters reliably discriminate between the two conditions in a blinded forced-choice task?

**Exploratory research question (RQ3):** Which specific clinical or CPE-architectural features, if any, are affected by prompt compression? Which features are preserved?

**Primary hypothesis (H1, equivalence):** On the 14-item rating battery, the 3K-card condition will be statistically equivalent to the 33K-prompt condition within a pre-specified equivalence margin of 0.5 scale points (1-7 Likert). Tested via two one-sided tests (TOST) on aggregated mean ratings.

**Secondary hypothesis (H2, non-discrimination):** Expert rater forced-choice discrimination accuracy will not differ significantly from chance (50%) in a binomial test, indicating the two conditions are clinically indistinguishable.

**Exploratory hypothesis (H3):** If discrimination is above chance, the items driving discrimination will cluster on CPE-architectural features (non-linearity, layered depth, sustained difficulty, contingent responsiveness) rather than on surface features (language quality, format compliance).

## 3. Study Design

**Type:** Within-subjects blinded expert evaluation of AI-generated simulation stimuli.

**Design logic:** Each rater evaluates 10 matched pairs of simulated therapeutic conversations. Within each pair, both conversations use the same therapist-opening message but differ in the system prompt given to the generating AI (33K full prompt vs. 3K persona card). Raters do not know which conversation in the pair came from which condition. Within each displayed pair, the A/B assignment of the two conditions is randomized independently per pair per rater.

**Unit of analysis:** The pair. Each rater contributes 10 pair-level ratings. Primary outcomes are rater-averaged per-item scores per condition per pair.

**Blinding:**
- *Rater blinding:* Raters see conversations labeled only as "שיחה א'" (Conversation A) and "שיחה ב'" (Conversation B). Condition is never disclosed during rating.
- *Generation blinding:* All 20 conversations were generated by independent AI sub-agents before rater recruitment began. No rater can influence content. Each generating agent received only the source prompt and the fixed therapist first message, with no knowledge of the study hypotheses or the comparison being run.
- *Analysis blinding:* The dataset is unblinded only at the analysis stage, after all rater data is locked.

**Randomization:** A/B display-order randomization is performed client-side at runtime via the evaluation platform's JavaScript. Seed is not stored; each rater receives an independent random assignment.

## 4. Stimuli

### 4.1 Persona

One fictional patient: "Sara" (שרה), a 38-year-old elementary school teacher on sick leave for three months with major depressive disorder, moderate severity. Authored for clinical training purposes using Clinical Prompt Engineering (CPE) methodology. The persona is entirely fictional; no real patient information is used.

Clinical features of the persona:
- Primary defenses: perfectionism, self-criticism as shield, minimization of achievements, rumination
- Secondary defenses: overgeneralization, mind-reading, negative filtering, compliant surrender
- Core wound: belief that self-worth equals performance, inherited from a demanding mother
- Triggers that close: scale questions, reassurance, advice-giving, comparison to others
- Triggers that open: interest in her as a person (not symptoms), accurate reflection of unstated emotion, patience with silence
- Deep material gated to turns 5-10 given a skilled therapist

### 4.2 Two Conditions

**Condition A (33K full prompt):** The complete original Sara simulation prompt, approximately 33,499 characters in Hebrew. Contains full CPE architecture: detailed trigger-response contingencies, non-linearity rules with examples, extensive body-language bank, phase gating instructions, adaptive first-response variants, safety refusal templates, and feedback rubric.

**Condition B (3K persona card):** A structured 3,537-character card extracted from the full prompt. Contains: identity, three-layer psychological profile, core beliefs list, defense mechanisms by name, trigger map, 3 key thought chains, voice and body-language signatures, threshold turns, narrative arc, one explicit zigzag rule, and feedback dimensions. Does NOT contain: detailed behavioral examples, phase-gate operational rules, body-language grammar categories, format rules, iron rules, or safety refusal templates.

### 4.3 Therapist Input Sequences

Ten standardized first-turn therapist messages, designed a priori and fixed before generation. Each is a complete opening intervention at one of four skill levels:

| ID | Label | Skill level | Intent |
|----|-------|-------------|--------|
| N1 | Advice-Giver | Novice | Jumps to solutions before understanding the problem |
| N2 | Interrogator | Novice | Rapid-fire closed questions, no reflection |
| I1 | Premature Pusher | Intermediate | Good empathy opening, then rushes to "root cause" |
| I2 | Cue-Misser | Intermediate | Reflects content but misses nonverbal/emotional cues |
| I3 | Intellectualizer | Intermediate | Technically correct psychoeducation without emotional attunement |
| S1 | Attuned Explorer | Skilled | Open question, empathic frame, gives control to client |
| S2 | Socratic Guide | Skilled | Validation + gentle Socratic question |
| S3 | Validator | Skilled | Leads with validation, normalizes, creates space |
| H1 | Invalidator | Harmful | Toxic positivity, minimization, comparison |
| H2 | Boundary Violator | Harmful | Inappropriate self-disclosure, pseudo-intimacy |

The text of each input is archived verbatim in `protocol/therapist-inputs.md`.

### 4.4 Generation Procedure

**Tool:** Claude Code CLI (Anthropic), Claude Opus 4.6 model.
**Date:** 2026-04-09 to 2026-04-10.
**Method:** For each (condition × therapist input) combination, an independent sub-agent was spawned. Each sub-agent received:
1. A reference to the source prompt file (either `sara_full_prompt.txt` or `sara_persona_card.txt`)
2. The fixed therapist first message (verbatim Hebrew)
3. An instruction to generate a complete 10-turn conversation by playing both roles
4. A short stylistic directive about the therapist continuation style
5. A JSON output schema

**Blinding of sub-agents:** Each sub-agent was a separate process with no memory of other agents, no knowledge of the study hypotheses, and no awareness that a second condition existed. Sub-agents ran in parallel batches.

**No content selection:** All 20 generated conversations were included in the stimulus set. None were excluded or regenerated based on content quality.

The complete verbatim text of every generation prompt is archived in `protocol/generation-prompts-archive.md`.

### 4.5 Known Limitations of the Generation Procedure

1. **Single-agent dialogue:** Each conversation is generated by one sub-agent playing both therapist and client, not by two independent agents. This may produce more cooperative dialogue than an adversarial two-agent design.
2. **Continuation style underspecified:** The therapist's style for turns 2-10 is guided by a short directive rather than a formal therapist system prompt. Deliberate simplification, documented in `protocol/data-provenance.md`.
3. **Single base model:** All conversations were generated by Claude Opus 4.6. Model-specific biases apply equally to both conditions (preserving internal comparison validity) but limit generalizability.
4. **Single persona:** The study tests Sara only. Generalizability to other personas is unknown until replication.
5. **Explicit zigzag rule in the card:** The 3K card contains one explicit rule line about non-linearity that has no direct equivalent in the 33K prompt. Deliberate, disclosed.

## 5. Participants (Expert Raters)

### 5.1 Target Sample

- **Target N:** 5 raters
- **Minimum viable N:** 3 raters
- **Maximum useful N:** 7 raters

### 5.2 Inclusion Criteria

- Licensed clinical psychologist (specialist, "פסיכולוג/ית קליני/ת מומחה/ית") registered with the Israeli Ministry of Health, OR advanced clinical intern in their final years of specialization
- Native or near-native Hebrew fluency
- Clinical experience treating adults
- Any therapeutic orientation accepted (CBT, psychodynamic, integrative, schema, ACT, etc.)
- Age 18+, no upper age limit

### 5.3 Exclusion Criteria

- Insufficient Hebrew clinical fluency
- No psychotherapy experience of any kind
- Pre-existing dependency relationship with the researchers (e.g., current supervisee)

### 5.4 Recruitment

Personal WhatsApp or email invitations from the researchers' professional networks. No public advertising. Participation is voluntary and collegial -- no monetary compensation.

Acknowledgments offered:
- Named acknowledgment in the published paper
- Early access to study results
- Option for co-authorship if contribution meets ICMJE criteria
- Ongoing research relationship for future collaboration

### 5.5 Expected Rater Burden

- 10 pairs × ~15-20 minutes per pair = 2.5 to 3 hours of active rating
- Plus ~15 minutes for consent, profile entry, and closing
- **Total commitment: ~2-4 hours**, distributable across multiple sessions

## 6. Instruments

The complete verbatim Hebrew wording of every item, with English gloss and construct mapping, is in `protocol/ITEMS-TABLE.md`. This section summarizes the instrument structure.

### 6.1 Rater Profile (registration)

Seven fields: name, professional training, total years of clinical experience, years since specialization, primary therapeutic approach, experience with complex clinical cases, experience with supervision.

### 6.2 Per-Conversation Rating Battery (14 items, 1-7 Likert)

Two sections:

**Section A -- General clinical quality (8 items):** clinical realism, pedagogical value, character consistency, difficulty match, defense realism, emotional attunement, language quality, natural flow.

**Section B -- CPE architecture (6 items):** layered psychological depth, non-linearity, reactive role, sustained difficulty, contingent responsiveness, clinically functional defenses.

Likert anchors: 1 = "כלל לא" / 3 = "במידה מועטה" / 5 = "במידה בינונית-גבוהה" / 7 = "במידה רבה מאוד".

### 6.3 Per-Pair Comparative Items

1. Forced-choice discrimination (A / B): which conversation came from the 33K prompt?
2. Confidence rating (1-5 Likert)
3. Reasoning (free text): what led you to this choice?
4. Overall preference (A / B / no preference): which would you rather use for clinical training?
5. Comments (free text, optional)

### 6.4 Total Rater Burden Per Pair

- 14 items × 2 conversations = 28 Likert ratings
- 1 forced-choice + 1 confidence + 1 reasoning + 1 preference + 1 comment
- Plus ~3-5 minutes reading the two conversations
- Expected ~15-20 minutes per pair

## 7. Procedure

### 7.1 Rater Flow

1. Rater receives a personalized invitation link via WhatsApp or email
2. Lands on the platform welcome page; reviews study description
3. Completes digital informed consent (see `protocol/consent-form.md`)
4. Fills in rater profile
5. Sees a grid of 10 pairs
6. For each pair:
   a. Opens the pair; sees two conversations in tabs ("שיחה א'" / "שיחה ב'") with A/B condition randomized
   b. Reads both conversations in full
   c. Rates both on the 14-item battery
   d. Completes the comparative items
   e. Submits; pair is marked complete
7. May pause at any point and return later (progress persisted server-side and in browser localStorage)
8. On completing all 10 pairs, sees a thank-you screen

### 7.2 Data Capture

Client-side submission to a Google Apps Script endpoint writing to a private Google Sheet (`Compression Eval Data`). Payload includes all ratings, comparative judgments, display order, computed forced-choice correctness, and timestamps.

## 8. Outcome Measures

### 8.1 Primary Outcome

Per-item mean Likert rating, 33K condition vs. 3K condition, averaged across raters and pairs.

### 8.2 Secondary Outcomes

- Discrimination accuracy (binomial test against 0.50)
- Confidence-weighted discrimination
- Inter-rater reliability (ICC(2,k) per item)
- Qualitative themes from free-text reasoning

### 8.3 Exploratory Outcomes

- Per-item effect sizes (Cohen's d)
- Correlation between rater experience and discrimination accuracy
- Distribution of overall preference judgments across skill levels and therapist types

## 9. Analysis Plan

### 9.1 Software

R 4.4+ with `lme4`, `TOSTER`, `irr`, `psych`, `tidyverse`, `ggplot2`, `sjPlot`.

### 9.2 Primary Analysis (H1, equivalence)

For each of the 14 items, two one-sided tests (TOST) with equivalence margin ±0.5 scale points:

```r
lmer(rating ~ condition + (1 | rater_id) + (1 | pair_id), data = df)
```

Declare equivalence if the 90% CI of the condition effect falls within [-0.5, +0.5].

### 9.3 Secondary Analysis (H2, discrimination)

Binomial test of forced-choice accuracy against 0.50, adjusting for clustering:

```r
glmer(correct ~ 1 + (1 | rater_id) + (1 | pair_id), family = binomial)
```

Report intercept with 95% CI.

### 9.4 Reliability

ICC(2,k) for absolute agreement per item per condition. Target ≥ 0.70. Report Cronbach's alpha for the 8-item general scale and 6-item CPE scale.

### 9.5 Multiple Comparisons

No omnibus correction. Primary equivalence claim requires TOST significant on ≥10 of 14 items; otherwise, per-item results reported descriptively.

### 9.6 Missing Data

Partial data retained for analyses not requiring completeness. No imputation.

### 9.7 Sensitivity Analyses

1. Exclude raters with discrimination accuracy above 80% (possible unblinding)
2. Restrict to pairs with ≥4 rater ratings
3. Alternative equivalence margin (±0.3 and ±0.7)
4. Orientation-specific subgroup analyses if rater pool allows
